A Conceptual Foundation for the Structure of Technical Practice
An analysis of purposeful operation and its primary driver — technique: the structured process by which an agent transforms, constructs, operates, and maintains material or informational artifacts to bring about desired states in the world.
Founding Definition

Technical practice can be characterized as the structured process by which an agent transforms, constructs, operates, and maintains material or informational artifacts to bring about desired states in the world.

Why Systematize Technique?

Every act of making, operating, or maintaining is saturated with structure. A surgeon executing a laparoscopic procedure and a compiler translating source code into machine instructions share something deeper than surface analogy — they both enact technique: a generalized, culturally transmitted body of knowledge regarding how to achieve a specific type of technical effect.

Yet the conceptual vocabulary for describing this structure is fragmented across engineering disciplines, computer science, and philosophy of technology. There is no unified schema that captures what a Technical Blueprint has in common with a Constitutive Technique, or how a Technical Constraint differs from a Technical Standard — and why that distinction matters for understanding why systems fail.

What This Taxonomy Captures

This project formalizes 33 concept types organized into 9 relation families — from the ontological substrate of Reality through which all intervention passes, to the teleological orientation of Technical Purpose that gives every artifact its ultimate reason for existence.

It distinguishes the Production Technical Object (the deployed end-product) from the Constitutive Technical Object (the intermediate component). It separates Technical Function (mechanistic input-output) from Technical Purpose (intended impact). It traces how Technical Feedback propagates through Maintenance to counter the entropic degradation that Reality imposes on all artifacts.

The result is not a classification for its own sake. It is a conceptual operating system — a foundation for organizing the ideas used to explain operation and praxis across diverse fields and tasks.

The Map Is Not the Territory.

This taxonomy is itself a technical artifact — subject to the same constraints of perspective, historical situatedness, and potential obsolescence it attempts to categorize. It does not replace the practice it describes. It renders it intelligible.
